2. MESSAGING FORMATS THAT WORK
-------------------------------

Carousel dominates everywhere. Robinhood runs carousel in 95% of ads, Kraken at near 100%, Crypto.com at 40+ of 44. This format clearly survives optimization — it lets competitors run multi-panel product tours, step-by-step benefit stacks, and visual comparisons. It is the workhorse of fintech paid social.

Video appears in longer-running formats at Crypto.com (612 days) and Coinbase (86 days), but video is not dominant by volume. Where video runs long, it tends to carry a single strong mechanic — a contest, a journey narrative, or a product demo — not a brand story.

Long-form copy outlasts short. The Crypto.com 612-day gamification ad and Kraken's 160-day recurring-buys carousel both use structured multi-benefit copy with emoji bullet points. Short declarative ads ("Trade at the speed of crypto") run shorter. Benefit-stacked, problem-solution copy sustains longer.

Direct-response beats brand-building on longevity. The ads with the longest run times lead with concrete incentives (win BTC, earn rewards, start with $10) rather than brand awareness. Cash App's "The way money should work" is the exception — a brand line — but it sits at only 49 days.


3. WHAT SPEED SHOULD LEARN
--------------------------

Use carousel as the primary format. Every optimized competitor has converged here. Speed should build modular carousel sequences for each segment — one for remittance, one for iGaming deposits, one for crypto-curious onboarding.

Anchor at least one ad to a concrete incentive mechanic. Crypto.com's longest-surviving creative is a reward loop (Missions to Diamonds to Mystery Box). Kraken ties trading to contest entries. Speed should test a similarly sticky hook — for example, a referral reward or first-transfer bonus — as the carousel narrative.

Benefit-stack copy with emoji structure outperforms headlines alone. Kraken's 160-day ad ("Set recurring buys... Automate your strategy... Stop guessing") and Robinhood's download ad use clean checklists. Speed should adopt this for zero-fee remittance and instant iGaming deposit messaging.

Selective creator use for trust, not just reach. Kraken's Kitboga choice was strategic — a scam-awareness creator lending credibility to a security message. Speed could find a remittance-community creator or a gaming streamer to do the same for its two core segments.


4. WHERE SPEED SHOULD DIFFERENTIATE
-------------------------------------

No competitor is owning remittance. Every brand in this dataset speaks to investors, traders, or debit card users. Zero-fee international transfers for workers sending money home is completely uncontested ad territory. Speed should run dedicated remittance carousels with real corridor messaging — "Send money to the Philippines in seconds, zero fees" — and own that emotional job-to-be-done.

iGaming instant deposits are invisible in this data. Not one competitor mentions gaming deposits, instant settlement, or casino/sportsbook use cases. This is a wide-open lane. INFERRED: competitors are avoiding regulatory risk or simply not targeting this segment. Speed should test direct iGaming-adjacent copy carefully but aggressively in permissive markets.

Simplicity framing is available but not owned. Coinbase tries ("simplify Bitcoin") but executes it as a generic journey narrative. No competitor has a sustained "this is the easiest Lightning wallet, full stop" creative thread. For crypto-curious users, Speed can own the "first Bitcoin transaction in under two minutes" angle — something none of the dataset brands have claimed in their longest-running copy.

Speed (the name) is an unused creative asset. Kraken writes "Trade at the speed of crypto" — but Speed Wallet IS the speed. Across all competitor copy, no brand owns literal transaction speed as identity. That intersection of brand name and product truth is differentiated positioning no competitor can replicate.
