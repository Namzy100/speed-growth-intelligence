The three most important findings this week are the dramatic shift in our acquisition mix toward organic channels, the efficiency gap between our two paid platforms, and a concerning retention anomaly on June 9th. Organic sources now represent nearly 86% of total installs with 31,999 users across all organic channels combined, while paid channels delivered just 3,314 installs. Apple Search Ads is significantly outperforming Facebook with an eCPI of $3.91 versus $6.55, and the Facebook Payday campaign specifically is running at $5.12 per install despite being our top paid performer by volume with 1,235 installs. Meanwhile, D1 retention dropped to 10.9% on June 9th after hovering consistently in the 23-30% range throughout the prior month.

Apple Search Ads is our most efficient paid channel right now, delivering installs at $3.91 compared to Facebook's $6.55. The brand exact match campaign is performing especially well at just $3.59 per install with 1,021 users acquired, suggesting strong intent from people already searching for Speed Wallet. The click-through rate is healthy at 7.9% with 3,725 clicks from 46,870 impressions, indicating our creative and targeting are resonating with high-intent audiences already familiar with our value proposition.

Facebook needs immediate budget review. At $6.55 per install overall and $6,325 spent on the Payday campaign alone, we're paying 67% more per user than Apple while targeting what should be a highly motivated remittance audience. The 6.1% click-through rate from 257,037 impressions suggests the creative is engaging, but conversion from click to install is weak. Either our landing experience isn't optimized for this audience or we're attracting low-intent clicks that don't convert.

I recommend pausing the Facebook Payday campaign immediately and reallocating that budget to scale the Apple brand campaigns. The Payday creative has proven it can drive awareness with over 15,000 clicks, but at $5.12 per install it's simply not competitive with Apple's sub-$4 efficiency. We should test the remittance messaging on Apple Search instead, targeting keywords around money transfer and international payments where user intent is already qualified.

D1 retention appears flat based on the seven-day trend showing a negligible 0.8% decline, but June 9th's 10.9% retention is a red flag that warrants investigation. This represents a 56% drop from the June 8th baseline of 24.7% and falls far outside normal variance. This could signal a technical issue with the app, a bad cohort from a specific source, or a tracking problem that needs immediate attention before we scale any paid spend further.
