The three most important findings this week are the dominance of organic and low-cost brand installs, the extreme inefficiency developing in Meta's experimental campaigns, and a concerning stability in day-one retention that masks what may be a quality versus quantity tension in our acquisition mix. Organic installs represent 66 percent of total volume at 27,972 users with zero reported cost, while our two brand campaigns on Google Ads and Apple Search Ads combined deliver 5,473 installs at a blended cost well below four dollars. Meanwhile, Meta's Cash Deposit campaign burned over one thousand dollars for just thirteen installs at an eCPI of $77.42, and our retargeting efforts are returning installs in the high teens to mid-twenties cost range, signaling serious targeting or creative fatigue.

Facebook is performing most efficiently right now, and it's worth noting that this efficiency lives almost entirely in a single campaign. The Payday Android Broad+ campaign delivered 1,563 installs at $3.17 eCPI with a click-through rate of 7.80 percent, making it our lowest-cost paid channel by a meaningful margin. This campaign is clearly resonating with the remittance sender segment, likely catching users around paycheck cycles when remittance intent peaks. The creative and timing alignment here is working, and this is where incremental budget should flow first.

Meta's experimental and retargeting inventory needs immediate attention. The Cash Deposit campaign is effectively non-functional at $77.42 per install, and both retargeting efforts are returning installs in the $19 to $24 range, roughly five to seven times the cost of our best-performing Facebook campaign. These campaigns have either exhausted their addressable audience, are running against poorly matched creative, or are targeting segments with low intent. A budget review should pull spend from these three campaigns immediately and either pause them or force a creative and audience reset before any additional dollar is committed.

The specific recommended action is to shift the entire Cash Deposit and retargeting budget into scaling the Payday Android Broad+ campaign while simultaneously launching a parallel iOS version of that same creative and timing strategy. The rationale is straightforward: we have a campaign performing at half the cost of our next-best paid channel with high engagement, and we're currently lighting money on fire elsewhere. Reallocating that roughly $1,500 in monthly waste into a proven winner should yield an additional 450 to 500 installs at sustainable cost, and testing the same payday-hook creative on iOS will tell us quickly whether the efficiency is Android-specific or segment-specific.

D1 retention is flat, with the recent seven-day average at 24.8 percent compared to 25.2 percent the prior week. The trend shows no meaningful movement, but the absolute level signals a potential onboarding or expectation mismatch. A quarter of users returning the next day is serviceable but not strong, especially given our zero-fee and instant-transaction hooks, which should create immediate utility and stickiness. Flat retention while volume grows suggests we're not yet degrading quality with scale, but we're also not improving the first-day experience enough to convert more installers into engaged users.
