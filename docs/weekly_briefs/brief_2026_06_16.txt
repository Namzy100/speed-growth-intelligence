This week's data reveals three critical findings. First, we're facing a cost-tracking blackout across our Google Ads channel, which drove 5,016 installs but shows $0 spend and $0 eCPI—this is a broken integration, not free traffic. Second, organic continues to dominate volume at 26,451 installs, representing 68% of all acquisition, which suggests strong word-of-mouth or brand pull but also concentration risk. Third, D1 retention is declining, dropping from 26.5% to 25.4% week-over-week, with notable volatility including a spike to 30.9% on June 13th that hasn't held.

Apple Search Ads is our most efficient paid channel right now, delivering 2,078 installs at $3.91 eCPI with the brand exact-match campaign performing especially well at $3.50 per install. The channel combines volume, cost efficiency, and high intent—users searching "Speed Wallet" are likely remittance or crypto-curious segments already aware of the value proposition. Facebook trails at $5.30 eCPI overall, though the Payday campaign is more competitive at $4.31.

Google Ads urgently needs a budget review, not because performance is poor but because we have zero spend visibility despite 5,016 installs and three top-ten campaigns including a 2,316-install brand campaign. This is a disconnected cost integration in the Adjust dashboard, meaning we're flying blind on what's likely our second-largest paid channel. We can't optimize what we can't measure, and right now we have no idea if Google Ads is profitable or burning cash.

Reconnect the Google Ads spend feed in Adjust immediately. With over 5,000 installs in 30 days, this channel represents meaningful scale, but without eCPI data we can't compare it to Apple or Facebook, can't set rational bids, and can't allocate budget intelligently across the three paid networks. If Google Ads is actually performing at Apple-level efficiency, we're likely underfunding it; if it's wasteful, we're overspending blind.

D1 retention is declining, down 1.1 percentage points over the past week. The trend sits around 25% with occasional spikes into the high twenties and low thirties, but the recent seven-day average of 25.4% vs. 26.5% the prior week signals a softening in day-one stickiness. This could indicate creative fatigue, segment drift, or onboarding friction as we scale—worth investigating cohort quality by channel and revisiting first-session experience, especially for mainstream users who may need more hand-holding than iGaming veterans.
